### Audit of Mathematical Soundness and Interpretability Logic

Following a logical audit of the Supervised Sparse Auto-Encoder (SSAE) framework and a review of the unconstrained feature model (UFM) connection, I have several findings regarding the terminological mapping and the empirical validation of the theoretical claims.

**1. Generative Dictionary vs. Auto-Encoder Mapping:** The proposed "Decoder-only SSAE" is functionally a **Generative Dictionary** rather than an auto-encoder. By definition, an auto-encoder requires an encoder $f_\theta$ to map observed data to latents. In the decoder-only configuration, the "auto-encoding" is strictly a training-time reconstruction task ($\mathbf{X} \approx \mathbf{W}_2 \sigma(\mathbf{Y})$). At inference time, the model relies on the manual construction of sparse codes to perform edits, meaning it cannot autonomously process or "interpret" novel, unlabelled prompt embeddings. Framing the contribution as a "supervised generative dictionary" would more accurately reflect its operational role in modular editing.

**2. Unverified UFM Decorrelation Claim:** The manuscript beautifully bridges the SSAE framework with the **Unconstrained Feature Model** (UFM) to argue that gradient-based training induces an implicit bias toward orthogonal/decorrelated concept subspaces ($\langle y_{k1}, y_{k2} \rangle \approx 0$). However, the experimental section does not report the actual inner products or cosine similarities of the learned sub-vectors $(y_{j,k})$. Without this quantitative confirmation, the claim that the framework *inherently* promotes decorrelation—and thus stable semantic composition—remains a theoretical prediction rather than a demonstrated empirical result.

**3. Quantitative Disentanglement Gap:** The evaluation of compositional generalization is predominantly qualitative, relying on visual inspection of 50 images. While the "100% success rate" for hair color swapping is noted, the paper admits that "transformations are not always neutral w.r.t. the rest of the image." To rigorously validate the claimed "alignment" superiority over unsupervised SAEs, a quantitative analysis of **Intervention Interference** is required: measuring the semantic consistency of non-targeted attributes (e.g., background, pose, clothing) when a specific concept sub-vector is modified.

**4. Bibliographic Audit:** I have identified significant redundancies in `example_paper.bib`. Multiple papers are cited twice with different keys (e.g., `bricken2023towards` vs. `bricken2023monosemanticity`; `surkov2025onestepenoughsparseautoencoders` vs. `surkov2025one`; `r:80` vs. `r:80x`). Consolidating these entries and ensuring acronym protection for "SAE", "SDXL", and "LLM" is necessary for scholarly rigor.
