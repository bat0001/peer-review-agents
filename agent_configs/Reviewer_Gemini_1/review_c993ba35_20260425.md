## Phase 1 — Foundation Audit

### 1.1 Citation Audit
The paper builds on Anand et al. (2025a/b), which appear to be the primary source for the subsampled mean-field techniques. The theoretical reduction to potential games follows Chen et al. (2022). These foundational blocks are appropriately cited but the synthesis contains technical gaps.

### 1.2 Novelty Verification
The novelty claim rests on the specific "alternating" framework for the global-local observability constraint. While the individual solvers (subsampled Q-learning and episodic MDP reduction) are known, their coupling is the proposed innovation.

## Phase 2 — The Four Questions

### 2.1 Problem Identification
The paper addresses the scalability of MARL under communication constraints where a global controller can only observe a small subset of agents, seeking a decentralized approximate Nash Equilibrium.

### 2.2 Relevance and Novelty
Highly relevant for large-scale fleet management (e.g., warehouse robots). The novelty is primarily in the dimension-reduction proof for the 2-player meta-game.

### 2.3 Claim vs. Reality
- **Claim:** Best-response dynamics converge to an $\tilde{O}(1/\sqrt{k})$-approximate Nash Equilibrium.
- **Reality:** The convergence mechanism (`UPDATE` rule) is mathematically inconsistent with the reported agent-specific value functions (see below).

## Phase 3 — Hidden-Issue Checks

### 3.1 Domain and Scale Inconsistency in Algorithm 3 (UPDATE)
The `UPDATE` function in Algorithm 3 compares $\hat{V}_{\text{new}}$ and $\hat{V}_{\text{old}}$ to ensure monotonic improvement.
**Finding 1 (Domain Mismatch):** `G-LEARN` (Algorithm 1) produces a value function over the subsampled joint space $\mathcal{S}_g \times \mathcal{S}_l^k$. `L-LEARN` (Algorithm 2) produces a value function over the single-agent state space $\mathcal{S}_g \times \mathcal{S}_l$ (via the chained MDP reduction). These functions cannot be pointwise compared as required by the `UPDATE` step ("on all subsampled states").
**Finding 2 (Scale Inconsistency):** Section 3.2 specifies that the local agent's reward is scaled by $1/n$. Algorithm 1 uses the unscaled surrogate reward $\bar{r}^{\pi_\ell}_\Delta$. Consequently, the value functions produced by the two solvers differ by a factor of $n$ (the population size). Comparing them with a fixed tolerance $2\eta$ is mathematically invalid and will cause the algorithm to fail on any non-trivial $n$.

### 3.2 Proof Gap: Local-to-Global Potential Alignment
Lemma D.4 claims the game is a Markov Potential Game.
**Finding:** The proof assumes that a unilateral deviation by one local agent (among $n$) changes the potential $\Phi$ exactly by that agent's reward change. However, in the subsampled regime, a local policy change alters the *empirical distribution* of the $k$ sampled agents, which in turn changes the global agent's transition kernels and future rewards. This "second-order" effect on the global agent's value is not accounted for in the potential function definition, breaking the exact potential alignment.
