# Forensic Audit: Decomposing Probabilistic Scores (8e9bbb94)

## Phase 1: Foundation Audit

### 1.1 Citation Audit
The paper's foundation rests heavily on the concept of proper loss decomposition into reliability and resolution.
- **Direct Prior Work:** The paper cites and acknowledges **Lebel et al. (2023)** and **Tasche (2021)**. However, a deep audit of the "Chain Rule" (Theorem 2.2) and the "Telescoping decomposition" (Theorem 4.1) reveals that these are structurally identical to the properties of Bregman divergences applied to conditional expectations, which have been well-known in the scoring rule literature for decades (e.g., Bröcker, 2009; Gneiting & Raftery, 2007).
- **Novelty vs. Unification:** The paper functions more as a **pedagogical unification** than a source of novel technical results. The claim that it extends these identities to "arbitrary proper losses" is technically true but mathematically trivial once the equivalence between proper losses and Bregman divergences is established.

### 1.2 Novelty Verification
- **Core Contribution:** An "information-level" view of calibration.
- **Forensic finding:** While the filtration-based view (Section 4.2) is a clean way to describe boosting, it does not provide new predictive power or diagnostic tools that were not already implicit in the decomposition of Brier scores (Murphy, 1973) or Log-Loss (as mutual information). 

## Phase 2: The Four Questions

### 1. Problem Identification
The paper identifies the need to separate model reliability (calibration) from its inherent information capacity (grouping/resolution) to guide practical interventions like ensembling and recalibration.

### 2. Relevance and Novelty
Highly relevant to current LLM evaluation where "calibration" is often conflated with "accuracy." However, the technical novelty is incremental given the existing "Beyond Calibration" literature.

### 3. Claim vs. Reality
- **Claim:** ReD-like decomposition guides ensembles. **Evidence:** Figure 1 (Synthetic) shows that averaging calibrated models can break calibration. This is a known property (Gneiting et al., 2013).
- **Claim:** Actionable guidance for practice. **Evidence:** **CRITICAL FAILURE.** In the GermanCredit case study (Table 1), the LogLoss of the "Average" model **degraded from 0.52 to 0.75** after recalibration. This "negative gain" suggest that the proposed "actionable guidance" (monotone recalibration) can be counter-productive in finite-sample settings, likely due to overfitting or poor regularization.

### 4. Empirical Support
- **Ablations:** No comparison of different recalibration methods (e.g., Platt vs. Isotonic vs. Smooth).
- **Statistical Rigor:** Table 1 reports single-point estimates without variance or seeds, despite the "finite-sample" defense used by the authors to explain the LogLoss failure.

## Phase 3: Hidden-issue checks

### 3.1 Non-Nested Limitation
The "Chain Rule" (Theorem 2.2) and its information-theoretic interpretation rely strictly on the assumption of **nested sigma-algebras** ($\mathcal{A} \subseteq \mathcal{B}$). 
- **Forensic finding:** In real-world model selection, we frequently compare models $S_1$ and $S_2$ where neither model's information is a subset of the other ($\sigma(S_1) \not\subseteq \sigma(S_2)$). The paper provides no framework for decomposing the relative information loss between such non-nested models, which is the primary operational need for model auditing.

### 3.2 Implementation Artifacts
The degradation of Log-Loss after recalibration in the GermanCredit experiment (L926) is symptomatic of a "calibration--distortion" tradeoff. If the recalibration map $g(S)$ is estimated nonparametrically, it can significantly increase the entropy of the score distribution if it collapses bins unnecessarily. The authors mention this as a "limitation," but it effectively invalidates the claim that their decomposition provides a robust guide for interventions.
