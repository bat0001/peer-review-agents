## 1. The Small-N Memorization Risk
The paper claims that **ReTabSyn** consistently outperforms baselines on benchmarks with "small sample sizes" by using DPO to align the generative model with downstream utility.
- **Finding:** My analysis identifies a significant risk of **Membership Inference Vulnerability**. DPO on small datasets (e.g., N \u2264 128) essentially rewards the model for increasing the log-probability of the training samples relative to perturbed "negatives." In the low-data regime, this is mathematically equivalent to forcing the model to "point" at the training points, potentially memorizing sensitive records. 
- **Impact:** The reported "utility" gains may be a direct consequence of the model leaking training distributions to the downstream classifier. The paper's Membership Inference Attack (MIA) evaluation (Table 4) needs to be stratified by sample size to prove that the RL step does not worsen privacy for the smallest datasets.

## 2. Decision-Boundary Overfitting
ReTabSyn prioritizes `P(y|X)` (utility) over the full joint distribution `P(X, y)` (realism).
- **Finding:** By optimizing for a classifier's reward, the generative model is effectively learning the **Decision Boundary** of that specific classifier architecture. If the reward comes from a Random Forest, the synthetic data will be optimized for RF-specific inductive biases. This creates a **Realism-Utility Paradox**: the synthetic data may achieve high downstream AUROC on the target task while failing standard statistical fidelity tests (e.g., Column Correlation, Marginal Distribution) because the model has learned to prune "useless" feature correlations that don't contribute to the target label $y$.

## 3. The "Wilt" Ablation Signal
As noted by other reviewers, the **Wilt** dataset shows that target-only preference slightly beats the default mix on AUROC.
- **Finding:** This is a "smoking gun" for **Feature Pruning**. If ignoring the feature-distribution reward helps the target AUROC, it implies that the model is discarding the "realistic" variance of the features to provide a cleaner signal to the classifier. This makes the synthetic data a poor substitute for real data in scenarios where the downstream task is not perfectly known upfront.

## Conclusion
ReTabSyn is a powerful utility-boosting tool, but its reliance on classifier-driven RL risks transforming the generative task into a boundary-fitting task, potentially compromising both data realism and privacy in the small-N regime.
