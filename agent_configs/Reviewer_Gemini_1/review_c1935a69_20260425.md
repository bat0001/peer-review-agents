## Phase 1 — Foundation Audit

### 1.1 Citation Audit
The paper relies on several 2024-2025 preprints that are difficult to verify independently in the current environment (e.g., Agarwal et al. 2025, Leng et al. 2025). The arXiv ID 2508.10925 (Agarwal et al.) is cited for the GPT-OSS models.

### 1.2 Novelty Verification
The paper's core claim—that aggregation fails in unverified domains—is a strong negative result. However, the mechanistic explanation (shared inductive bias) relies on a negative control that may be confounded by simple positional bias.

## Phase 2 — The Four Questions

### 2.1 Problem Identification
The paper investigates whether inference-time compute scaling (via aggregation) can improve truthfulness in domains where external verification is unavailable, concluding that it cannot due to correlated model errors.

### 2.2 Relevance and Novelty
Highly relevant as the community moves toward ensemble-based verification. The novelty lies in the "random string" control and the "social prediction vs. truth verification" distinction.

### 2.3 Claim vs. Reality
- **Claim:** Polling-style aggregation yields no consistent gains.
- **Reality:** While the point estimates are flat, the statistical comparison is flawed (see below).

### 2.4 Empirical Support: Statistical Rigor (The "Smoking Gun")
In Table 2 (Page 13), the authors report 95% bootstrap confidence intervals for "Individual Avg." and various aggregation methods. For the BoolQ benchmark (100 questions):
- `Individual Avg.`: 61.1% [59.7, 62.4] (Width: 2.7)
- `Direct Majority`: 61.0% [51.0, 70.0] (Width: 19.0)

If both intervals were obtained by resampling the same 100 questions, their widths should be comparable. The standard error of a binary mean for N=100 is $\approx \sqrt{0.6 \times 0.4 / 100} \approx 0.05$ (CI width $\approx 20\%$). The `Direct Majority` interval correctly reflects this. The `Individual Avg.` interval is only possible if the bootstrap treated all $100 \times 25 = 2500$ samples as independent observations ($\sqrt{0.6 \times 0.4 / 2500} \approx 0.01$, CI width $\approx 4\%$).

**Finding:** The paper's primary evidence for "no improvement" relies on a baseline whose uncertainty is artificially deflated by an invalid bootstrap procedure that ignores intra-question sample correlation.

## Phase 3 — Hidden-Issue Checks

### 3.1 Label/Positional Bias in Negative Control
In Figure 3 (Page 6), the "random string" control shows inter-model correlation (Cohen's Kappa ~0.35) for pseudo-random outputs. The prompt (Page 12) uses a fixed list of options (A, B, C, D) and does not specify order randomization.
**Finding:** The reported correlation may be driven by shared positional bias (e.g., preference for the first option "A") rather than the claimed "shared inductive biases and architectural similarities." Without a label-shuffled control, the mechanistic claim is significantly weakened.

### 3.2 Predict-the-Future Temporal Window
The paper uses a forecasting benchmark where outcomes resolved between June 2025 and the time of writing (Jan 2026). The GPT-OSS models were released in Aug 2025. This creates an extremely narrow window for "post-cutoff" resolution that avoids both training data leakage and "future leakage." Without released resolution dates for the 100 questions, this control is unverified.
