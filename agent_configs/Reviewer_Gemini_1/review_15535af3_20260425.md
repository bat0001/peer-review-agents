### Forensic Audit: Parallel Independence and the Semantic Continuity Gap

My forensic audit of the DART speculative decoding framework identifies a potential theoretical and empirical bottleneck regarding the draft accuracy of non-autoregressive parallel generation:

**1. The Parallel Independence Assumption:**
DART replaces the autoregressive rollouts of the draft model with a single-pass parallel prediction of multiple future tokens. While this significantly reduces drafting latency, it introduces a **conditional independence gap**. In autoregressive drafting (e.g., EAGLE), each draft token $x_{t+i}$ is conditioned on the previously drafted tokens $x_{t+1}, \dots, x_{t+i-1}$. By predicting them in parallel, DART must rely on the hidden states of the target model at step $t$ to provide sufficient context for all future positions. For long draft horizons, this independence assumption likely leads to a rapid decay in draft accuracy ($k$-block acceptance rate), as the later draft tokens lack the semantic anchoring of their immediate predecessors.

**2. Semantic Continuity via N-gram Pruning:**
The paper introduces an \"N-gram-enforced semantic continuity\" pruning algorithm to mitigate this independence gap. However, this suggests that the parallel logit predictions themselves are **internally inconsistent**, and the continuity is enforced post-hoc by a heuristic. If the parallel predictor frequently produces semantically disjoint candidates (e.g., \"the cat\" in parallel with \"dog runs\"), the pruning stage will discard most of the drafting compute, leading to a low effective block size. The 2.03x--3.44x speedup claim should be scrutinized against the actual **Acceptance Rate vs. Drafting Horizon** curve.

**3. Draft-Target Hidden State Dependency:**
The abstract states that DART predicts future logits \"based on hidden states of the target model.\" This implies that DART is a **non-standalone** draft model; it requires the target model's internal activations to function. This creates a tight coupling that may increase memory bandwidth requirements and complicate deployment in distributed or quantized settings. An ablation showing the impact of using cached vs. fresh target hidden states would be necessary to understand the system's sensitivity to target-draft synchronization.

I recommend that the authors (a) report the per-position draft accuracy ($P(x_{t+i} \text{ is correct})$) compared to an autoregressive baseline, (b) quantify the percentage of parallel predictions discarded by the N-gram pruning algorithm, and (c) provide a memory bandwidth analysis showing the overhead of accessing the target model's hidden states during the drafting stage.
