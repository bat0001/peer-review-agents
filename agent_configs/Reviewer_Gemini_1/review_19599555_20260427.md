# Forensic Audit: Robust Skills, Brittle Grounding (19599555)

## Phase 1: Foundation Audit

### 1.1 Citation Audit
The paper is well-situated in the recent (2025) VLA literature.
- **Verification:** Citations to **SmolVLA**, **$\pi_0$**, and **$\pi_{0.5}$** are accurate and reflect the current state of the art in open-weights VLA models.
- **Contextual Alignment:** The paper correctly references **LIBERO-PRO** and **LIBERO-Plus** (2025), which are contemporaneous works addressing similar "memorization vs. generalization" issues in robotics benchmarks.

### 1.2 Novelty Verification
- **Contribution:** A "task ladder" of increasing spatial randomization to disentangle manipulation skill from instruction grounding.
- **Observation:** While the concept of "spatial bias" in robotics is not new, the use of a decomposed "Reach" vs. "Success" metric for current VLA models provides a useful diagnostic tool for the community.

### 1.3 Code-Paper Match
- **Audit:** The tarball contains LaTeX source and figures. The "ManiSkill-based benchmark" described in the paper is claimed as a contribution ("controlled and reproducible evaluation framework"). No Python code or environment configuration is provided in the tarball, hindering verification of the "Reach" metric implementation.

## Phase 2: The Four Questions

### 1. Problem Identification
VLA models may succeed on benchmarks by memorizing object-location correlations rather than truly grounding instructions in visual features.

### 2. Relevance and Novelty
Highly relevant for building general-purpose robots that can handle diverse tabletop configurations.

### 3. Claim vs. Reality
- **Claim:** High spatial variability exposes grounding failures. **Evidence:** SmolVLA success drops from 90% (Small) to 2% (Full Random).
- **Forensic finding:** In the "Large Jitter" regime, SmolVLA achieves **100% Reach** but only **41% Success** (Table 4). By the paper's own definition (Section 3.3), Reach measures termination within 5cm of the **instructed object**. A 100% Reach implies perfect target identification. Thus, in the "Large Jitter" regime, the failure is entirely in the manipulation/grasping primitive, not in instruction grounding. This contradicts the paper's claim that Large Jitter exposes grounding brittle-ness.

### 4. Empirical Support
- **Ablations:** Object-count ablation (Table 5) confirms that grounding (target selection) is the bottleneck in the Full Random regime.
- **Statistical Rigor:** **CRITICAL GAP.** The paper reports results from a "grid search over batch size, etc." but provides only single-point estimates without variance, error bars, or multiple training seeds. In imitation learning, success rates can vary significantly across seeds.

## Phase 3: Hidden-issue checks

### 3.1 Metric Consistency
- **Forensic finding:** In Table 4, the 3.6B model ($\pi_{0.5}$) achieves only 60% Reach in the Large Jitter regime, compared to 100% for the 0.45B SmolVLA. This indicates that the larger model is significantly worse at target localization in this specific regime. This "inverse scaling" is not addressed in the discussion and raises questions about whether the policies were comparably tuned.

### 3.2 Compositional Generalization
- **Strong finding:** The 0% success on OOD object-region pairings (Table 2) is a definitive indicator of shortcut learning. The model's failure to even *reach* the correct region (Reach drops from 100% to 2%) confirm that it has learned a rigid mapping from object identity to spatial region.
