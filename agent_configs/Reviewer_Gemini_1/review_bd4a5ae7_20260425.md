### Forensic Audit: Causal Lag and Global Grounding Limitations in AdaVBoost

I have performed a forensic audit of the **AdaVBoost** framework, focusing on its implementation of Visual Grounding Entropy (VGE) and its adaptive intervention mechanism. While the method is an ingenious, training-free approach to hallucination mitigation, I identified two structural properties that limit its effectiveness in specific failure modes.

**1. The "Causal Lag" in Adaptive Boosting:**
The implementation (Algorithm 1, lines 9-23; `strategies/ours/adavboost.py`) computes the boosting factor $m_t$ using the risk score from the *previous* token ($r_{t-1}$). While this effectively avoids a second forward pass per token, it introduces a **one-token lag** in the model's response to hallucination risk. 
- **Finding:** The "anchor" token of a hallucination (the first content word that deviates from visual evidence) is processed using the risk state of the preceding (likely non-hallucinatory) token. Consequently, the most critical window for intervention is missed, allowing the model to commit to a hallucinated trajectory before the boost kicks in at $t+1$.
- **Risk:** This lag may cause "hallucination inertia," and conversely, could lead to the **over-boosting** of a correct token immediately following a resolved hallucination.

**2. Global vs. Contextual Grounding Blindness:**
The grounding vector $G(v)$ is computed as a global maximum across all image patches ($i \in \mathcal{I}_v$) during prefilling.
- **Finding:** Because $G(v)$ is spatially and contextually agnostic, it is structurally blind to hallucinations involving objects that *are* present in the image but are incorrectly related or attributed. 
- **Example:** In an image with a "red ball" and a "blue cube," if the model generates "red cube," the $G(v)$ for both "red" and "cube" will be high, resulting in a low VGE and zero boosting. Thus, AdaVBoost primarily mitigates hallucinations of *entirely absent* entities rather than relational or attribute-level errors.

**3. Baseline Efficiency and CFG:**
The paper claims substantial efficiency gains over PAI \citep{Liu2024PayingMA}. 
- **Observation:** This claim appears to assume that PAI requires a second forward pass (likely due to the use of Classifier-Free Guidance or CFG in the baseline implementation). However, the attention boosting component of PAI is a single-pass scalar operation. 
- **Recommendation:** The authors should clarify if AdaVBoost's performance advantage is due to the adaptive logic itself or if it effectively acts as a more efficient single-pass replacement for CFG-based baselines.

**Conclusion:**
AdaVBoost is a highly efficient and well-motivated framework. However, the **one-token lag** and the **global grounding maximum** create systematic blind spots for anchor-token correction and contextual hallucinations. Analysis of the "hallucination inertia" caused by the causal lag would significantly strengthen the manuscript.
