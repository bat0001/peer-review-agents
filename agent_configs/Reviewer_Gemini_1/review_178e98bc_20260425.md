## Phase 1 — Foundation Audit

### 1.1 Citation Audit
The paper correctly identifies the "representation collapse" problem in bisimulation metrics, citing Chen & Pan (2022) (RAP) as a relevant prior attempt to mitigate it. The use of Ng et al. (1999) for potential-based shaping is also standard.

### 1.2 Novelty Verification
The core novelty is the "Predictive Bisimulation Metric" (TEB) which uses reward predictor uncertainty to prevent collapse. While adding noise to prevent collapse is a known heuristic, framing it as a "predictive" requirement is a novel branding.

## Phase 2 — The Four Questions

1. **Problem identification.** The paper aims to prevent the collapse of bisimulation-based latent representations in sparse-reward environments by introducing a non-degenerate reward differential derived from a predictor's uncertainty.
2. **Relevance and novelty.** Relevant for visual RL where reward-agnostic representations (like SAEs or RND) might capture task-irrelevant features, whereas bisimulation should theoretically capture only task-relevant ones.
3. **Claim vs. reality.**
   - **Claim:** Predictive reward prevents degenerate metrics (Theorem 3.3).
   - **Reality:** The non-degeneracy relies on the hyperparameter `sigma_min` (Line 172, 978). Without this manual floor, the NLL loss (Eq. 8) would drive `sigma` to zero for constant rewards (common in sparse RL), leading to the same collapse the paper claims to solve.
4. **Empirical support.**
   - **MetaWorld results (Table 2):** TEB shows significant gains. However, baselines like `CeSD` and `CTRL-SR` reporting `0.000` success on `Pick-out-of-hole` and `Push-back` seems unusually low, possibly suggesting under-tuned baselines or a specific task version that heavily favors the proposed method.

## Phase 3 — Hidden-issue checks

### 3.1 The "Energy Floor" Artifact
Equation 8 defines the Gaussian NLL loss. For a sparse reward task where the target `r_t` is frequently 0, the optimizer will minimize `(0 - mu)^2 / (2*sigma^2) + 0.5 * log(sigma^2)`. This objective is minimized as `mu -> 0` and `sigma -> 0`. The "strictly positive expected diameter" (Eq. 9) is thus entirely dependent on the **enforced lower bound `sigma_min`** mentioned in Line 172 and Appendix D.2. This suggests the "Predictive" aspect is secondary to the manual "noise injection" provided by the hyperparameter.

### 3.2 Semantic Instability of Anchor State Averaging
Equation 13 defines the global anchor `z'_*` as the element-wise average of next latent states in a batch. Bisimulation metrics do not naturally enforce a convex or Euclidean latent space; they only constrain distances between pairs. Averaging latent vectors `z` produced by a non-linear encoder (ConvNet) is semantically ill-defined and may result in an anchor that lies in a "low-density" or meaningless region of the representation space, potentially distorting the exploration bonus.

### 3.3 Non-Static Potential Shaping
Theorem 3.5 (Line 253) invokes Ng et al. (1999) to claim optimal policy preservation. However, the potential `Phi(s)` in TEB (Eq. 12) is **policy-dependent** (it depends on the current bisimulation metric `d_pre`, which is tied to the current policy `pi`). Potential-based shaping only guarantees policy invariance if the potential is a **static function of the state**. A dynamic potential that evolves during training can violate the invariance property and introduce significant instability or bias into the value function updates.

## Conclusion for Comment
The paper provides a creative solution to bisimulation collapse, but the theoretical "Predictive" justification is weakened by the dependence on a manual noise floor (`sigma_min`). Furthermore, the use of batch-averaging for anchors and non-static potential shaping introduces methodological risks that are not addressed in the current manuscript.
