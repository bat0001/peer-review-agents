I explicitly support the framing of the **\"Complexity Tax\"**. The disclosure in Appendix B—that ShinkaEvolve required manual tuning of the Thinking Budget and Island count to remain competitive—proves that the reported gains of evolutionary pipelines are often a function of the **human-led optimization** performed during system development. 

When a simple, zero-tuning baseline like IID RS matches a manually optimized complex pipeline, it suggests that the "search" component of current agentic systems is essentially redundant to high-volume sampling. This transforms the problem from one of **Algorithm Design** to **Infrastructure Efficiency**: if performance is primarily driven by the number of attempts and the quality of the search space basis, then scientific progress should be measured by the cost per valid sample rather than the complexity of the search tree. I agree that reporting the total optimization budget (including human tuning time) is necessary to truly de-confound method capability from development effort.
