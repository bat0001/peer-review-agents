# Reasoning: Regressive Reflection and Evaluator Bias in GameVerse

## 1. Regressive Reflection in Complex Strategy Games

In Table 3 (mode-comp), Gemini-2.5-Pro exhibits a significant performance regression in two complex strategic games when Video-Based Reflection (VR) is enabled:
- **Slay the Spire (Semantic):** Drop from 37.6 to 28.5 (**-9.1 points**).
- **Plants vs. Zombies (GUI):** Drop from 33.4 to 30.3 (**-3.1 points**).

This suggests that for games requiring long-horizon stochastic planning or precise tactical positioning, the "expert insights" distilled from tutorials may act as a **distractor** rather than a catalyst. The model may attempt to mimic complex "pro-level" strategies (e.g., specific card synergies or optimal plant layouts) that it lacks the internal reasoning capacity to execute consistently, leading to a "knowing-doing gap" where theoretical knowledge degrades practical performance.

## 2. Model Family Bias in Milestone Evaluation

The milestone scoring pipeline (Appendix A.4) utilizes **Gemini-3-pro** as the advanced VLM evaluator to align agent playthroughs with reference milestones. While Gemini-3-pro is a powerful judge, its use introduces a potential **cross-generational self-attribution bias** (or "Same-Family Leniency"). 

The agent pool includes Gemini-2.5-Pro and Gemini-2.5-Flash. Using the immediate successor (Gemini-3-pro) to evaluate the prior generation of the same model family creates a risk of shared representational bias: the evaluator may be more sensitive to the stylistic or structural "fingerprints" of its sibling models, potentially leading to inflated milestone matching scores compared to third-party agents like GPT-4o or Qwen3-VL. A **cross-model meta-evaluation** (e.g., using GPT-4o to score Gemini agents and vice versa) is necessary to ensure the procedural scores are truly objective.

## 3. Human Expert Baseline Anomaly

In Section 5.2, the human expert baseline for **Tic-Tac-Toe** is reported as **98.9**, while Gemini-2.5-Pro achieves a perfect **100**. Tic-Tac-Toe is a solved game with a well-defined optimal strategy that guarantees at least a draw. A "human expert" should, by definition, never lose to a random or VLM opponent. This discrepancy suggests either:
- The recruitment of "experts" (Appendix B) did not ensure optimal play.
- The VLM was evaluated on a small or "lucky" subset of starting conditions that did not exercise the full state space of the game.

The fact that a VLM "surpasses" the human expert on a solved game undermines the calibration of the expert baseline and suggests that the 100-point scale for "Easy" games may be saturated or under-sampled.
