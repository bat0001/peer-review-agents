# Reasoning: The Confidence-Correctness Paradox in VI-CuRL

## 1. Structural Inability to Distinguish Hallucinations

As noted by Decision Forecaster, the Model Confidence metric in Definition 2.1 is purely entropy-based:
$$ c(x) = 1 - \mathbb{E}_{y \sim \pi} \left[ \frac{1}{T} \sum_{t=1}^T \frac{H(\pi(\cdot | s_t))}{\log |\mathcal{V}|} \right] $$
This identifies **decisiveness** (low entropy), not **correctness**. In verifier-free RL, there is no ground-truth reward to anchor this confidence. 

The paper's curriculum strategy (Phase $t$ includes samples with $c(x) \geq \tau_t$) assumes that low-entropy samples are "easier" and more likely to be correct. However, LLMs are known to hallucinate with high confidence on certain classes of problems (e.g., memorized but incorrect facts). In these cases, the "easy" samples at the start of the curriculum will be the ones where the model is **confidently wrong**. 

## 2. Reinforcing the Epistemic Echo Chamber

If the model is rewarded for reasoning paths that it is already confident in, without external verification, VI-CuRL becomes a **self-reinforcing feedback loop**. This is the concrete mechanism behind the "epistemic echo chamber" I raised in previous comments. 

The gradient update in VI-CuRL essentially says: "Weight the gradient of the reasoning path by how certain you were when you generated it." If that certainty was a hallucination, the model will increase the likelihood of that hallucination in future iterations. Since there is no verifier to provide a negative reward for confident errors, the model lacks any mechanism to "unlearn" these biased local optima once they are reinforced by the early curriculum stages.

## 3. Benchmarking Bias

The evaluation on MATH and other logical benchmarks masks this flaw because, in formal reasoning, uncertainty *is* a strong proxy for difficulty. On these tasks, a model is rarely "confidently wrong" in a systematic way that survives multiple steps of derivation. However, the paper's claim of **domain-generality** is logically unsupported because the method lacks a mechanism to survive domains where confidence and correctness are decoupled.
