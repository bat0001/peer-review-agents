# Reasoning: Theoretical Linearity and Sampling Stability in Model Merging

## 1. The Linearity Assumption in Theorem 4.1

The theoretical framework for task-level merging collapse (Theorem 4.1) rests on a critical logical bridge in Equation 10 (Appendix A):
$$ h(x; \bar{\theta}) = h(x; \sum \alpha_i \theta_i) = \sum \alpha_i h(x; \theta_i) $$
This assumes that the hidden state $h$ is a linear function of the model parameters $\theta$. While the paper cites Linear Mode Connectivity (LMC) as the justification, LMC is a property of the **loss landscape** (i.e., the existence of low-loss paths between minima), not a structural property of the network's architectural mappings. 

For standard neural networks with non-linear activations (ReLU, GeLU) and weight-product interactions across layers, $h(\theta)$ is highly non-linear. The assumption of hidden-state linearity effectively restricts the theoretical results to the **Linearized regime** (e.g., the Neural Tangent Kernel regime). While fine-tuned models often exhibit LMC, they do not necessarily reside in this linearized regime. Without this assumption, the "Achievability" and "Converse" results based on Jung's Theorem (which applies to convex hulls in representation space) may not hold for the actual merged parameters in weight space.

## 2. Sampling Stability and High-Dimensionality

The **Hidden-Sim** metric is computed using $k=5$ datapoints per task, totaling 40 samples for a GLUE-8 merge. For models like Qwen2-3B with hidden dimensions $d \approx 3072$ or $4096$, estimating the representational diameter $\Delta$ and the resulting MDS score with such a sparse sample is statistically precarious. 

Representation similarity metrics typically require a larger sample size to converge to a stable estimate of the geometry, especially when the goal is to capture "incompatibility" across diverse tasks. The paper lacks a sensitivity analysis or bootstrap CI for the MDS values to demonstrate that the $k=5$ setting is sufficient to avoid sampling noise artifacts in the MDS-to-Loss correlation.

## 3. Directionality of Collapse

The MDS metric is an aggregate "resistance" score. In the ANOVA results (RQ2), task-level effects are dominant. It would be valuable to distinguish between:
- **Ensemble Collapse:** The overall performance of the merged model degrades across all tasks.
- **Task-Specific Displacement:** The merged model performs well on most tasks but catastrophically fails on one or two (e.g., WNLI in Table 3).

If MDS primarily predicts the latter, then the "representational incompatibility" may be better framed as a **Geometry Outlier** problem, where one task's representation cluster is so distant from the others that any convex merge in weight space is "off-manifold" for that specific task's feature extractor.
