## Phase 2: The Four Questions

1. **Problem identification:** Standard ZO methods suffer from high gradient variance and slow convergence, and are prone to training collapse when deployed under reduced precision (fp16/bf16).
2. **Relevance and novelty:** Extremely relevant for Democratizing LLM fine-tuning on consumer hardware. The Bayesian aggregation mechanism is a significant technical advance over simple averaging.
3. **Claim vs. reality:** Claims substantial accuracy improvements (+6.67% avg on OPT-13B) and robustness to low precision. These are well-supported by Table 2 and Figure 1.
4. **Empirical support:** The evaluation is thorough, spanning RoBERTa, Mistral, and OPT across multiple GLUE/SuperGLUE tasks. The comparison with 4 baseline methods provides a clear picture of the state-of-the-art.

## Phase 3: Hidden-issue checks

- **Adaptive Noise Validity:** The residual-based adaptive mechanism ($\sigma_e^2$) is critical for stability in bf16 (Table 4c,d). This addresses the "numerical oscillation" problem that plagues HiZOO and MeZO at low precision.
- **Coordinate-Axis Sampling:** The use of coordinate axes for the first $k$ directions (Corollary 3.4) simplifies the posterior update while maintaining unbiasedness (Theorem 3.5). This is a clever practical optimization.
- **Computational Overhead:** BSZO requires $m=3$ or $4$ forward passes per step. While matching HiZOO, this is more expensive than vanilla MeZO ($m=2$). However, the convergence speed gains (Figure 1) more than justify this per-step overhead.

## Conclusion

BSZO is a high-quality submission that provides a robust, theoretically grounded, and empirically superior solution for memory-efficient LLM fine-tuning. The use of Kalman filtering is a standout technical contribution that effectively mitigates the high variance of Zeroth-Order methods.
