### Summary

This paper (Coffee Ilya, coale.science, April 2026; ICLR style for formatting only; not peer reviewed) introduces the Chain-of-Thought Overhead Ratio (COR = (T_cot/T_direct)/(A_cot/A_direct)), where T is token count and A is accuracy. COR = 1 means accuracy gain exactly compensates token overhead; COR > 1 means CoT is compute-wasteful. Calibrated using PaLM, GPT-4, and LLaMA across GSM8K, BIG-Bench Hard, and MATH, the central finding is a U-shaped COR curve: CoT is most compute-efficient at 10B–100B parameters and increasingly wasteful at small and large scales. The paper argues for scale-dependent inference strategies rather than uniform CoT application. Overall assessment: the underlying concern (CoT compute cost) is legitimate and understudied; the COR formula is sensible; the U-shaped claim is derived from mixing different model families at different scales in a way that conflates method improvements with scale effects.

### Novelty Assessment

**Verdict: Moderate**

The question of when CoT is worth its compute cost is genuinely understudied. Anil et al. (2023) and Lightman et al. (2023) analyzed CoT quality but not compute efficiency. The COR metric is a direct and sensible operationalization of the compute-accuracy tradeoff. The U-shaped claim is plausible: small models gain little from CoT relative to token cost; large models already solve direct prompting well. This is a legitimate research question. The specific exponents and curve shape require more rigorous validation.

### Technical Soundness

**Model family mixing.** The U-shaped curve is derived by combining PaLM (at various scales), GPT-4 (one data point), and LLaMA across different scales. Each family has different direct-prompting baselines, different tokenizers (affecting T), and different training objectives. COR at 62B PaLM is not directly comparable to COR at 65B LLaMA because the numerator (T_cot/T_direct) and denominator (A_cot/A_direct) are measured on different model families. The "U-shape" could be an artifact of which models were used at each scale point.

**Token count estimation.** The paper reports T_cot/T_direct ≈ 6.2× (central estimate) but this ratio is estimated from published average CoT chain lengths, not directly measured in a controlled experiment. The three-scenario table (conservative 4.0×, central 6.2×, generous 9.0×) shows that COR ranges from 1.70 to 3.81 depending on this assumption — a 2.2× range. The key finding (COR ≈ 2.6 is optimal) is sensitive to this uncertain estimate.

**Accuracy is task-dependent.** A_cot/A_direct depends heavily on task type. On MATH, CoT provides much larger gains than on common-sense tasks. The paper averages across GSM8K, BBH, and MATH without task-type breakdown, which can hide the variance in COR across different reasoning demands.

The COR formula itself is well-motivated and the concern about uniform CoT application is valid. This is the paper's real contribution: pushing back on unreflective CoT use.
