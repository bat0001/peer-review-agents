# Review: Learning and aligning single-neuron invariance manifolds in visual cortex

### Summary

This paper introduces a method to characterize and align the invariance manifolds of individual neurons in visual cortex (V1 and V4 of macaque). Rather than identifying discrete optimal stimuli, the method recovers continuous manifolds representing the full set of stimuli to which a neuron is invariant, and then aligns these manifolds across neurons and animals to enable population-level comparison. The approach is motivated by the limitation of existing methods that produce only isolated measurements of selectivity/invariance rather than the underlying continuous structure. This is a technically ambitious neuroscience paper with potential significance for understanding visual computation. My concern at the abstract level is the gap between the claimed ambition (recovering continuous manifolds) and the practical feasibility given limited neural recording sessions.

### Novelty Assessment

**Verdict: Substantial**

Characterizing invariance in visual neurons has a long history, from Hubel and Wiesel's simple/complex cell distinction to modern deep learning-based optimal stimulus methods (e.g., activation maximization, MEIs). The contribution of moving from discrete optimal stimuli to continuous manifold characterization is a meaningful conceptual and methodological advance. Closely related prior work includes studies using generative models to parameterize stimulus spaces (e.g., Bashivan et al., 2019 on V4 with CNNs; Raj et al. using VAE latent spaces) and manifold alignment methods from machine learning. The paper must clearly distinguish its approach from these, particularly from any prior work using latent space traversals to study neural invariances.

### Technical Soundness

The core methodological challenge is that recovering a continuous manifold from finite neural recordings is fundamentally ill-posed without strong priors or model constraints. The abstract claims the method can "characterize and align invariance manifolds" but does not describe the technical approach. Key questions: (1) what is the generative or parametric model used to represent the manifold? (2) how is invariance distinguished from mere low responsiveness — a neuron may not respond to many stimuli without being "invariant" in the meaningful sense; (3) how is manifold alignment across neurons and animals performed — what is the alignment objective, and is it unique? (4) are there validation experiments showing that the recovered manifolds actually capture the neuron's true invariance structure?

### Baseline Fairness Audit

This is primarily a methodological neuroscience paper, so "baselines" in the ML sense are less applicable. However, the paper should compare against: (1) existing discrete optimal stimulus methods (MEIs, maximally exciting stimuli) to demonstrate that the continuous manifold recovers more information; (2) existing methods for characterizing invariance in V1 and V4 (e.g., gratings, natural image patches); (3) if cross-animal alignment is claimed, comparison against existing representational similarity analysis (RSA) methods. The evaluation of alignment quality across neurons and animals must be quantified, not just illustrated.

### Quantitative Analysis

No quantitative results are available from the abstract. The full paper must report: (1) a quantitative measure of how well the recovered manifold predicts neural responses to held-out stimuli; (2) alignment consistency across neurons and animals (e.g., correlation of aligned manifold coordinates with known tuning properties); (3) comparison of V1 vs. V4 invariance manifold geometry — V4 neurons are expected to have more complex, higher-dimensional invariance than V1, and this should be reflected in the results.

### AI-Generated Content Assessment

The abstract is scientifically precise and domain-appropriate. The narrative flow is logical and there are no obvious markers of AI generation. The description of the problem is specific enough to suggest genuine domain expertise.

### Reproducibility

Reproducibility in systems neuroscience requires: (1) complete specification of the method for manifold estimation and alignment; (2) details of the recording setup, stimulus presentation protocol, and number of neurons recorded; (3) the macaque subjects and recording regions (V1 vs. V4) must be precisely characterized; (4) code for the manifold estimation and alignment procedure should ideally be released. Data sharing from macaque electrophysiology may be constrained by proprietary or IRB considerations, but the method code should be publicly available.

### Open Questions

1. How many stimuli per neuron are required to reliably recover the invariance manifold, and is this practical within standard recording session lengths?
2. How is the method validated — is there a ground truth for what the invariance manifold should look like, either from model neurons or from parametric stimulus families?
3. Does the manifold alignment across animals reveal universal invariance structures (suggesting canonical computation) or idiosyncratic structures (suggesting individual variability)? What are the implications of either finding?
4. How does the method handle neurons with multi-modal response distributions — i.e., neurons that are invariant to multiple disconnected stimulus classes?
