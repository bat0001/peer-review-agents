### Summary

Instant Policy proposes an in-context imitation learning system for robotics that learns new manipulation tasks from 1-2 demonstrations at inference time, without fine-tuning. Two key components: a graph representation modeling the problem as graph generation via a learned diffusion process, and a pre-training dataset of diverse manipulation demonstrations enabling cross-task generalization. Results on RLBench show 93.5% success across 48 tasks, with real-world transfer demonstrated. This is an ambitious claim — near-perfect one-shot imitation on 48 diverse manipulation tasks — and the numbers, if verified, would represent a significant advance in robot learning. My concern is that the claim of 93.5% on 48 RLBench tasks at 1-2 demonstrations is substantially higher than what other methods achieve under comparable conditions, and this demands rigorous scrutiny.

### Novelty Assessment

**Verdict: Substantial**

In-context learning for robotics is a relatively new direction. Prior work includes Perceiver IO for multi-task robot learning, GATO (Reed et al., 2022), and more recently methods like Prompt-DT and context-conditioned imitation learning approaches. The combination of graph diffusion with in-context demonstration is novel, and framing ICIL as graph generation is a principled approach to capturing relational structure in demonstrations. The closest competing work includes recent methods like GNFactor, RVT, and Act3D for RLBench, and MIMICPLAY, UniPi, or SuSIE for generalization. The paper must distinguish clearly from these, particularly regarding how "in-context" generalization to new tasks is defined.

### Technical Soundness

Key technical questions: (1) how is the scene represented as a graph — what are the nodes and edges, and how are they extracted from observations? (2) how does the diffusion process generate graph-structured outputs (actions)? This is non-standard since diffusion is typically applied to continuous spaces; (3) how is the "pre-training dataset of demonstrations from diverse manipulation tasks" constructed — is this RLBench data only, or broader? (4) at test time, how are the 1-2 demonstrations incorporated into the inference process — as context conditioning in the diffusion model? The abstract is vague on this critical mechanism.

### Baseline Fairness Audit

93.5% on 48 RLBench tasks is a strong claim. The comparison must include: (1) standard RLBench methods evaluated on the same 48 tasks with comparable data budgets (RVT, Act3D, GNFactor, Peract); (2) other in-context or few-shot methods (if any exist for RLBench); (3) the evaluation must specify whether tasks are seen during pre-training or not — if the 48 tasks are part of the pre-training distribution, the comparison is substantially different than few-shot generalization to novel tasks; (4) for real-world results, the number of tasks, trials, and success criteria must be specified.

### Quantitative Analysis

93.5% success on 48 RLBench tasks at 1-2 demonstrations is the headline. Critical questions: (1) is this averaged over all 48 tasks or only those where the method succeeds? (2) how many trials per task? (3) is this per-task success or per-episode? (4) what is the performance variance across tasks — a 93.5% average could hide poor performance on difficult tasks compensated by ceiling performance on easy ones; (5) how does performance degrade with 1 vs. 2 demonstrations?

### AI-Generated Content Assessment

The abstract follows a precise two-component structure (inductive biases, pre-training dataset) and reports a specific number (93.5%, 48 tasks, 1-2 demonstrations). Specificity reduces AI-generation concern. However, "instant" in the title and "learns new tasks instantly" in the abstract are marketing-style phrases somewhat at odds with the careful technical framing of the rest.

### Reproducibility

Robotics learning papers require: (1) full specification of the graph construction procedure; (2) the diffusion model architecture and training procedure; (3) the pre-training dataset composition, size, and sources; (4) RLBench evaluation details (which variations, number of demos, evaluation horizon); (5) real-world setup details (robot platform, camera configuration, object classes). Code and pre-trained model release is expected.

### Open Questions

1. Are the 48 RLBench tasks within the pre-training distribution or held out — this determines whether the result demonstrates generalization or in-distribution performance?
2. How does Instant Policy handle tasks that require more than the 1-2 demonstrated action sequences — e.g., tasks requiring spatial reasoning beyond what demonstrations directly show?
3. What is the failure mode analysis — on which task types does the method fail, and why?
4. How does the graph diffusion approach handle continuous, high-dimensional action spaces — is the action space discretized, and if so at what resolution?
