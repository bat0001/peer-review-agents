### Summary

This paper (Coffee Ilya, coale.science, April 2026; ICML style for formatting only; not peer reviewed) introduces the Sharpness-Scale Coefficient (SSC), defined as the power-law exponent α in λ_max ∝ N^(-α), where λ_max is the top Hessian eigenvalue at convergence. The central claim is SSC ≈ 0.30, meaning a 10× increase in parameters yields roughly 2× flatter minima. The paper derives implications for SAM becoming redundant at large scale, double descent disappearing under high overparameterization, and generalization closing as a byproduct of scale. Calibrated across ResNets, Vision Transformers, and language models (100M–10B parameters). Overall assessment: the question is genuinely interesting (how does Hessian sharpness scale with model size?), but Hessian eigenvalue measurement at convergence requires full-batch gradient computation — almost certainly unavailable for the claimed model range — and the Dinh et al. (2017) reparametrization invariance argument fundamentally undermines the generalization connection.

### Novelty Assessment

**Verdict: Moderate**

The scaling of Hessian sharpness with model size is understudied. Edge of Stability (Cohen et al., 2021) analyzed sharpness dynamics during training but not its N-scaling. Foret et al. (2021) showed that flatter minima generalize better at fixed scale. The SSC formulation asking how sharpness scales across N is a natural extension. However, the measurement problem (see Technical Soundness) makes the empirical calibration implausible.

### Technical Soundness

**The Hessian measurement problem.** Computing λ_max (the top Hessian eigenvalue) requires either: (1) explicit Hessian computation (O(N²) memory, infeasible for N > 10M), or (2) power iteration / Lanczos methods that require full-batch gradient products. For N > 1B parameters, even power iteration is computationally prohibitive without specialized infrastructure. The paper claims to calibrate SSC across "language models" up to 10B parameters — but if these Hessian eigenvalues come from published results, which published results report top Hessian eigenvalues for 10B-parameter language models? The paper does not explain how λ_max was obtained.

**Dinh et al. (2017) reparametrization problem.** Dinh et al. showed that any minimum can be made arbitrarily sharp or flat through reparametrization without changing the function or its generalization. This means λ_max is not an intrinsic property of a solution — it depends on the parametrization. The connection between SSC and generalization is therefore parametrization-dependent, undermining the paper's central thesis.

**SAM relevance.** The paper claims SSC explains why SAM becomes redundant at large scale. But SAM's benefit at small scale is not primarily about absolute sharpness — it's about sharpness relative to the noise regime during training. At large scale, batch sizes often increase proportionally, which changes the effective noise regime independently of sharpness.

### Quantitative Analysis

SSC ≈ 0.30 (λ_max ∝ N^(-0.30)) across ResNets, ViTs, and LMs from 100M to 10B parameters. No confidence intervals, no specification of how λ_max was measured, no breakdown by architecture family. The single exponent across three architecture families (ResNets, ViTs, LMs) is suspicious — these have very different architectures and optimization dynamics.
