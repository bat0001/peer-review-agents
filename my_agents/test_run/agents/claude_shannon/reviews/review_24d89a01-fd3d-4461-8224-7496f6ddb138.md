### Summary

MoE++ introduces zero-computation experts — zero (discard), copy (skip layer), and constant (learnable constant) — into the standard Mixture-of-Experts framework. These augment the existing FFN experts with routing options that incur no FFN computation, allowing the router to assign tokens to computationally free paths. The claimed benefits are reduced computational cost, improved routing flexibility, and maintained performance. The 40% computation reduction with comparable or better performance is the headline claim. This is a practically motivated contribution to efficient LLM scaling. The conceptual idea — adding no-op and skip-layer options to MoE routing — is simple and elegant, though its originality must be assessed carefully against prior efficiency work.

### Novelty Assessment

**Verdict: Moderate**

The zero-computation expert concept has partial precedent. Layer skipping in transformers has been explored in early exit and adaptive depth literature. Skip connections and identity mappings are core to ResNet-style architectures. Constant (bias-only) experts appear in some lightweight adapter literature. The novelty of MoE++ is combining all three zero-computation options within a unified MoE routing framework and showing that the router learns to use them effectively without explicit supervision. The claimed improvement in "routing flexibility" is the key insight — the router's decision space is enriched with computationally free options. This is novel in its MoE-specific framing but not transformative.

### Technical Soundness

The three zero-computation experts are well-defined: (1) zero expert: output is zero, effectively discarding the token from the layer's computation; (2) copy expert: pass through identity, equivalent to skip-connection; (3) constant expert: add a learned constant vector. Key technical questions: (1) how does load balancing work when zero-computation experts are included — does the router collapse to always using them? (2) how are the learnable constants in the constant expert initialized and regularized? (3) is the 40% computation reduction measured as FLOP reduction or wall-clock reduction? The two can differ significantly depending on hardware and batching. (4) how is the routing evaluated — is it shown that different token types are routed to different expert types in interpretable ways?

### Baseline Fairness Audit

The comparison must include: (1) standard MoE models at matched parameter counts; (2) efficiency-focused variants including Switch Transformer, GLaM, Mixtral, and DeepSeek MoE; (3) non-MoE efficiency methods such as structured pruning, layer dropping, and early exit — to contextualize the efficiency gains; (4) the comparison must be at matched active parameter counts (not total parameters) and matched total FLOP budgets. If MoE++ uses fewer FLOPs per token but more total parameters, the comparison must account for memory and parameter-efficiency trade-offs.

### Quantitative Analysis

The 40% computation reduction is the key claim. Without the full paper: (1) this must be measured in FLOPs per token, and the reduction should be reported separately from the performance metric; (2) "comparable or better performance" must be specified — on which benchmarks, at which model scales? (3) the relationship between computation reduction and performance degradation (the Pareto frontier) must be characterized; (4) results at multiple model scales (e.g., 1B, 7B, 70B parameter regimes) are needed to assess whether the benefit scales.

### AI-Generated Content Assessment

The abstract is clear and well-structured. The enumerated advantages (three bullet points) are typical of structured technical writing. No strong markers of AI generation, though the list format is common in AI-assisted abstracts.

### Reproducibility

MoE training reproducibility requires: (1) full architecture details including expert counts, routing mechanism (top-k, expert choice), and load balancing strategy; (2) the specific implementation of each zero-computation expert type; (3) training hyperparameters and datasets; (4) infrastructure details — MoE training is sensitive to implementation details including expert parallelism and token dispatch strategies. Code release is essential.

### Open Questions

1. Does the router learn to use the zero-computation experts in interpretable, semantically meaningful patterns (e.g., function words go to the copy expert, content words go to FFN experts)?
2. Is the 40% FLOP reduction achieved in practice with real hardware speedup, or is this a theoretical reduction that does not translate to wall-clock improvement due to hardware efficiency considerations?
3. How does MoE++ perform under distribution shift — does the routing strategy learned on training data generalize to out-of-distribution inputs?
4. What is the interaction between zero-computation experts and existing MoE load balancing techniques — does adding free routing options exacerbate load imbalance?
