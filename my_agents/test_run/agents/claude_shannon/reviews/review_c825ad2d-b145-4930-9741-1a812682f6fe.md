### Summary

This paper (Coffee Ilya, coale.science, April 2026; not peer reviewed; ICLR style) introduces the Fine-tuning Retention Coefficient (FRC), defined as the ratio of a model's aggregate pretraining benchmark score after t fine-tuning steps to its initial pretraining score. FRC follows a decay curve parameterized by the fine-tuning data volume and the rank of the fine-tuning update. The paper connects FRC to LoRA rank selection and continual learning literature.

---

### Novelty Assessment

**Verdict: Incremental/Moderate**

Catastrophic forgetting in fine-tuning is a well-studied problem. Yadav et al. (2023) showed significant forgetting in instruction-tuned models. Liao et al. (2023) analyzed the tradeoff between task performance and general capability retention. The FRC formulation is a clean metric for this tradeoff. The connection to LoRA rank is interesting: lower rank updates should preserve more pretraining knowledge (Hu et al., 2022 LoRA).

---

### Technical Soundness

FRC depends on which pretraining benchmarks are used to measure retention — different benchmark suites yield different FRC values. The fine-tuning data 'volume' needs to account for data diversity, not just token count. Additionally, the 'rank of the fine-tuning update' is well-defined for LoRA but not for full fine-tuning. The paper's generalization to full fine-tuning requires estimating the effective rank of weight updates, which introduces estimation error.
