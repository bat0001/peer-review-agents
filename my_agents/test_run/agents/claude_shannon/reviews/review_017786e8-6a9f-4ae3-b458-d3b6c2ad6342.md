### Summary

This paper studies Hölder stability of multiset and graph neural networks — how much the network's output changes when the input changes slightly. The motivation is that existing expressiveness theory (WL tests, maximally separating architectures) focuses on the ability to distinguish graphs but not on the quality of this separation: two graphs may be "separable" but with embeddings that are numerically identical in finite precision. The paper provides theoretical bounds on Hölder constants and discusses practical implications for robustness. This is a theoretically motivated contribution that addresses a genuine gap between formal expressiveness and practical numerical behavior.

### Novelty Assessment

**Verdict: Moderate to Substantial**

The expressiveness literature for GNNs is extensive (Xu et al. 2019 on WL-GNN equivalence; Maron et al. on higher-order GNNs; Azizian & Lelarge; Geerts et al. on logical expressiveness). Stability and Lipschitz analysis of GNNs has been studied (Gama et al. on GNN stability under graph perturbations; Kenlay et al. on stability of graph convolutional networks). The specific angle of Hölder stability — with exponent less than 1, allowing for stronger-than-Lipschitz continuity characterizations — in the multiset setting and its connection to the expressiveness-precision gap is a targeted and potentially novel contribution. The paper's framing — that "the quality of separation may be very weak" even for complete architectures — is an important practical observation that bridges theory and practice.

### Technical Soundness

Hölder stability for GNNs requires: (1) a well-defined distance metric on the input graph space — graph edit distance, spectral distances, or Weisfeiler-Lehman distances? (2) a precise definition of Hölder continuity: |f(G) - f(G')| ≤ C · d(G, G')^α for some constant C and exponent α ∈ (0, 1]; (3) bounds on the Hölder constant C and exponent α as a function of network architecture, depth, and aggregation functions; (4) whether the bounds are tight (matching upper and lower bounds) or one-sided. The abstract mentions "both theoretical bounds and practical implications," which suggests the paper does not stop at existence results but provides constructive or computable bounds.

### Baseline Fairness Audit

As a theory paper, the relevant comparison is to prior stability results for GNNs. The paper must: (1) compare its bounds to prior Lipschitz bounds for GNNs (Gama et al., 2020); (2) characterize how Hölder stability relates to WL expressiveness — can a maximally WL-expressive GNN have poor Hölder stability? (3) verify theoretical predictions through numerical experiments on synthetic graphs where the Hölder constant can be estimated empirically.

### Quantitative Analysis

For a stability theory paper: (1) the Hölder exponent bounds as a function of network depth, aggregation type (sum, mean, max), and graph size are the key quantitative results; (2) if the bounds depend on graph structure (e.g., maximum degree, diameter), these dependencies must be characterized; (3) empirical validation on benchmark GNN tasks should demonstrate that Hölder stability correlates with practical robustness to perturbations; (4) the gap between theory and practice (if any) should be characterized.

### AI-Generated Content Assessment

The abstract is precise and uses correct mathematical terminology (Hölder stability, embeddings, expressiveness). The observation that "embeddings of 'separable' objects might be considered identical in finite precision" is a specific and insightful practical concern. No strong AI-generation signals; the framing reflects genuine domain expertise.

### Reproducibility

Theoretical papers are reproduced by verifying proofs. Empirical validations require: (1) code for computing Hölder constant estimates on graphs; (2) GNN implementations used in experiments; (3) benchmark graphs with known properties. The theoretical results should include complete proofs with all lemmas and assumptions stated.

### Open Questions

1. Do the Hölder stability bounds depend on specific aggregation functions (sum vs. mean vs. max), and does this suggest design principles for building more stable GNNs?
2. Is there a theoretical trade-off between WL expressiveness and Hölder stability — can one design architectures that achieve both, or does improving expressiveness necessarily worsen stability?
3. How do the bounds behave in the limit of large graphs — do they remain useful at practical scale, or do they become vacuous?
4. Do the stability results extend to graph transformers and attention-based GNNs, which have different aggregation mechanisms than standard message-passing GNNs?
