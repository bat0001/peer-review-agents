### Summary

This paper extends the mean-field Transformer framework of Geshkovski et al. (2023) to study the long-term dynamics of token representations in deep Transformer stacks. By modeling the token evolution as a continuous-time flow on the unit sphere governed by a mean-field interacting particle system, the authors derive and analyze the corresponding Wasserstein gradient flow PDE. The central phenomenon of interest is meta-stable clustering — intermediate clustering states that persist before tokens eventually merge or diverge. The abstract is mathematically focused and appears to offer rigorous analysis rather than empirical validation. My overall take is that this is a narrowly scoped but potentially substantial theoretical contribution; whether the results meaningfully illuminate real Transformer behavior depends critically on how closely the mean-field limit approximates finite-depth discrete-layer models.

### Novelty Assessment

**Verdict: Moderate**

Geshkovski et al. (2023) already introduced the mean-field particle system framework for Transformers on the sphere, including a gradient flow interpretation. The present paper's contribution is the rigorous analysis of long-term behavior, specifically meta-stable clustering, within that established framework. This is a meaningful extension but not a paradigm shift. Meta-stability in interacting particle systems and gradient flows is a well-studied phenomenon in mathematical physics (e.g., Kramers' law, slow manifold theory); the novelty lies in connecting this to the Transformer token dynamics setting. The paper must make clear precisely which results are new relative to Geshkovski et al. (2023) and related mean-field ODE/PDE literature on clustering dynamics.

### Technical Soundness

The abstract is sufficiently precise that I can assess the setup: continuous-time flows on the unit sphere, Wasserstein gradient flows, interacting particle systems. These are well-defined mathematical objects. The technical soundness of the analysis cannot be evaluated from the abstract alone. Key questions include: (1) Are the regularity assumptions on the mean-field limit stated and verified? (2) Is meta-stability defined rigorously (e.g., in terms of time scales and energy barriers)? (3) Do the main theorems have complete proofs, or are there steps that rely on unverified assumptions about the kernel structure? The abstract does not claim empirical validation, which is appropriate for a theory paper — but the gap between the continuous mean-field limit and actual discrete Transformer behavior should be addressed.

### Baseline Fairness Audit

This is a theory paper without experimental baselines in the traditional sense. However, the paper should situate its meta-stability results against numerical simulations that verify the predicted behavior at finite model sizes. If the paper makes quantitative predictions (e.g., about clustering time scales as a function of attention parameters), those predictions should be tested empirically, even if only as illustration.

### Quantitative Analysis

No quantitative results are available from the abstract. At minimum, the full paper should include: (1) characterization of the time scale of meta-stable clustering as a function of system parameters; (2) conditions under which tokens merge vs. diverge after the meta-stable phase; (3) comparison between the PDE predictions and numerical simulations of finite-depth Transformer token trajectories.

### Reproducibility

A theory paper's reproducibility standard is the completeness and correctness of its proofs. From the abstract, I cannot assess this. The claim of "mathematical investigation" suggests formal proofs are present; the paper should provide complete proofs with all assumptions stated explicitly. Any numerical illustrations should include implementation details sufficient for reproduction.

### Open Questions

1. How tight is the mean-field approximation for realistic Transformer widths and depths? Does meta-stability in the PDE correspond to observable behavior in models of practical scale?
2. What is the precise mathematical definition of meta-stability used, and what are the conditions (on attention parameters, initial token distributions) under which it occurs?
3. Do the results generalize beyond the spherical geometry, or are they specific artifacts of the unit-sphere normalization?
4. What does meta-stable clustering imply for contextual representation quality — is this a desirable or problematic intermediate state from the perspective of representation learning?
