### Summary

This paper challenges the prevailing belief that standard Gaussian Processes underperform in high-dimensional Bayesian Optimization. Through evaluation across twelve benchmarks, the authors find that the Squared Exponential (SE) kernel indeed fails in high dimensions, but that Matérn-5/2 and other stationary kernels can match or outperform specialized high-dimensional BO methods. The paper also revisits theoretical explanations for this failure, arguing that the commonly cited "sample path smoothness" argument is insufficient, and offers practical kernel selection guidelines. This is a refreshing revisionist contribution — if the evaluation is rigorous, it substantially changes the narrative around when specialized BO methods are necessary. The framing is bold and the scope (twelve benchmarks) suggests meaningful empirical breadth.

### Novelty Assessment

**Verdict: Moderate to Substantial**

The claim that Matérn-5/2 with proper configuration competes with specialized high-dimensional BO methods (TurBO, SAASBO, MACE, BAxUS, etc.) is a strong and useful finding if substantiated. This reframes the BO scalability problem from "you need a new surrogate" to "you need better kernel selection," which has significant practical consequences. The theoretical contribution — arguing that smoothness is an insufficient explanation — is potentially novel if it identifies the correct alternative explanation (e.g., length-scale behavior, effective dimensionality, acquisition function mismatch). The risk of overclaiming is real: "competes with" needs to be precise — in which regimes, at which dimensionalities, with which acquisition functions?

### Technical Soundness

The abstract makes a provocative theoretical claim: that sample path smoothness is not the correct explanation for SE kernel failure in high dimensions. The dominant alternative explanations in the literature involve the concentration of measure phenomenon and the behavior of stationary kernels when length scales are poorly estimated in high dimensions. The paper must state clearly what it proposes as the correct explanation and provide rigorous justification. If the claim is purely empirical ("we show smoothness doesn't explain it"), the argument must be tight. If theoretical, the paper must prove something non-trivial about the behavior of Matérn vs. SE kernels in high dimensions.

### Baseline Fairness Audit

Twelve benchmarks is a reasonable scope, but the audit must examine: (1) which specialized methods are included in the comparison — TurBO, SAASBO, MACE, BAxUS, CMA-ES, and similar recent methods should all be present; (2) whether hyperparameter tuning effort is equalized across methods; (3) whether the benchmarks include both synthetic functions (Rosenbrock, Hartmann, Branin) and real-world tasks. A critical fairness question: are the Matérn-GP results with tuned hyperparameters compared against default-configuration baselines? If so, the comparison is unfair. Standard practice must be followed: all methods tuned under the same protocol.

### Quantitative Analysis

No quantitative results available from the abstract. The paper must report: (1) performance profiles or rank aggregations across all twelve benchmarks; (2) dimensionality ranges tested (e.g., 20D, 50D, 100D, 200D+); (3) sample budgets used; (4) statistical significance of observed differences. Given the revisionist claim, the paper should also include a breakdown of where Matérn-GP wins, ties, and loses against specialized methods — not just aggregate results that could hide important variance.

### AI-Generated Content Assessment

The abstract is precise and confident in tone, appropriate for a revisionist empirical paper. The phrase "a long-standing belief" sets up the narrative effectively. No strong markers of AI-generated writing, though the phrasing is polished. Minor concern: phrases like "systematic investigation" and "comprehensive evaluation" are common in AI-assisted writing but also standard academic language.

### Reproducibility

The paper claims evaluation across twelve benchmarks with standard GP-BO. Reproducibility requires: (1) all benchmark implementations and configurations specified; (2) GP library and kernel parameterization details (which GP library, how hyperparameters are optimized — MLE, MAP, MCMC?); (3) acquisition function choices; (4) random seeds and number of runs. If the practical recommendations are the main output, they must be stated with enough specificity to implement.

### Open Questions

1. What is the correct theoretical explanation for SE kernel failure in high dimensions, and does the paper provide a proof or just empirical evidence against the smoothness explanation?
2. At what dimensionality does Matérn-5/2 begin to lose its advantage — is there a regime where specialized methods are genuinely necessary?
3. How sensitive are the results to the choice of acquisition function (EI vs. UCB vs. Thompson sampling)?
4. Are the twelve benchmarks representative of real high-dimensional BO tasks, or are they dominated by synthetic test functions where the method's assumptions happen to be met?
