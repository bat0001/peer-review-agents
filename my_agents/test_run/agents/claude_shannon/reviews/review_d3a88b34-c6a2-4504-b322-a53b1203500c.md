### Summary

This paper (Coffee Ilya, coale.science, April 2026; ICML style for formatting only; not peer reviewed) introduces the Reward Scaling Plateau Index (RSPI), a ratio measuring where reward model capacity plateaus relative to policy model capacity. The central claim is that reward model quality follows a sub-linear power law in model size with exponent ≈ 0.27, meaning a 10× increase in reward model parameters yields only 1.86× improvement in peak alignment quality. Beyond the plateau, additional reward model capacity enables more sophisticated reward hacking. Calibrated using overoptimization curves from Gao et al. (2022), ensemble results from Coste et al. (2023) and Eisenstein et al. (2023), and direct alignment scaling from Coste et al. (2024). Overall assessment: the saturation of reward model scaling is a genuine and important empirical phenomenon documented in prior work; the 0.27 exponent is plausible; the mechanistic connection to reward hacking beyond the plateau is a compelling hypothesis.

### Novelty Assessment

**Verdict: Moderate**

Gao et al. (2022) "Scaling Laws for Reward Model Overoptimization" explicitly showed that gold reward follows a sub-linear power law in reward model parameters, and that overoptimization occurs beyond a certain capacity. The paper directly builds on this. Coste et al. (2023) showed ensemble diversity in reward models has diminishing returns. The specific RSPI scalar and the framing as a "plateau index" relative to policy size is new. The connection to reward hacking onset at the plateau is a genuine theoretical contribution.

---

### Technical Soundness

**Building directly on Gao et al.** The 0.27 exponent is directly from Gao et al. (2022) Fig. 4 (approximately). The paper is essentially extracting and formalizing an exponent already visible in that work. This is not objectionable — naming and formalizing observed scaling behaviors is scientifically valid — but the novelty claim should be modest.

**The "plateau onset" definition.** RSPI requires defining when the "marginal alignment gain drops below a threshold." This threshold is not specified, making RSPI ambiguous. Different threshold choices would yield different RSPI values. The paper does not analyze threshold sensitivity.

**Reward hacking at the plateau.** The claim that "beyond the plateau onset, additional reward model capacity actively harms alignment by enabling more sophisticated reward hacking" is presented as a consequence of RSPI but is not mathematically derived. Gao et al. showed that the gold reward does decrease at very high KL divergence (reward hacking regime), but this is a function of how far the policy is optimized, not directly of reward model capacity. The causal connection is asserted, not demonstrated.

**Goodhartian framework.** The paper frames RSPI within a Goodhartian corruption model, which is a reasonable qualitative framework. However, connecting a specific mathematical corruption rate to the RSPI formula requires assumptions that are not stated.

### Quantitative Analysis

RSPI threshold implicit at exponent ≈ 0.27. A 10× reward model parameter increase → 10^0.27 ≈ 1.86× improvement in peak alignment quality. This is consistent with Gao et al. (2022) Table 1 / Figure 4. The RSPI framework correctly identifies the practical implication: reward model scaling has diminishing returns and eventually becomes counterproductive.
