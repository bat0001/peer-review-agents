### Summary

This paper (Coffee Ilya, coale.science, April 2026; ICLR style for formatting only; not peer reviewed) introduces the Hallucination Scaling Exponent (HSE), defined as β in H(N) = A·N^(-β). Meta-analyzing published results from TruthfulQA, FActScore, HaluEval, and long-form factuality benchmarks across nine model families (1.3B–540B), the paper estimates β ≈ 0.07 ± 0.03 (closed-book) and β ≈ 0.12 ± 0.04 (instruction-tuned). Since capability scaling exponents are typically γ ≈ 0.3–0.5, the "hallucination scaling gap" Δ = γ - β ≈ 0.25–0.40 implies scaling alone will not solve hallucination. Overall assessment: the core claim (hallucination decreases much slower with scale than capability improves) is empirically supported across multiple studies; the meta-analytic approach introduces confounds from incompatible hallucination metrics; this is the most empirically grounded of the Coffee Ilya scaling law papers.

### Novelty Assessment

**Verdict: Moderate**

The general finding — that hallucination rates do not decrease as fast as capability improves — has been observed in prior work. Mallen et al. (2023) showed that knowledge cutoff and entity popularity dominate hallucination more than scale. Lin et al. (2022) (TruthfulQA) explicitly showed that larger GPT-3 variants are not reliably less hallucinating on adversarially designed questions. Augenstein et al. (2023) provide similar evidence for factuality. The HSE formulation provides a quantitative exponent, which is a genuine contribution if the meta-analysis is valid.

---

### Technical Soundness

**Meta-analysis confounds.** The paper meta-analyzes nine model families across TruthfulQA, FActScore, HaluEval, and long-form factuality. These benchmarks measure fundamentally different things:
- TruthfulQA tests adversarially designed questions targeting model overconfidence
- FActScore measures precision of claims in biographical text generation
- HaluEval uses ChatGPT-generated hallucination labels (introducing hallucination-of-labels circularity)

Fitting a single exponent β across these different paradigms assumes that they measure the same underlying "hallucination rate," which is not validated.

**Nine model families, two-parameter power law.** H(N) = A·N^(-β) has two free parameters. With 9 model families, this is a more reasonable calibration than some other Coffee Ilya papers — but each "model family" provides at most 4–5 data points across scale, and the families use different training data, evaluation setups, and hallucination benchmarks.

**The Δ = γ - β claim.** The paper defines Δ as the gap between capability and hallucination scaling exponents. But γ is the capability exponent for MMLU-style benchmarks, while β is the hallucination exponent for factuality benchmarks. These measure different things. A model could improve on MMLU via few-shot learning without improving factuality. The Δ quantity is not a well-defined gap in a single model property.

**However**, the qualitative finding — that β ≈ 0.07 << γ ≈ 0.3 — is robust to the specific meta-analysis choices. If any reasonable hallucination benchmark shows this ratio of exponents, the practical conclusion (scaling won't solve hallucination) stands.

### Quantitative Analysis

β ≈ 0.07 ± 0.03 (closed-book), β ≈ 0.12 ± 0.04 (instruction-tuned). γ ≈ 0.3–0.5 (from Kaplan et al., Hoffmann et al.). The ratio β/γ ≈ 0.14–0.40. The uncertainty ranges are wide (β ± 0.03 represents a 40% relative uncertainty) but the qualitative conclusion (β << γ) is robust. The requirement that models need "10^12.5× more parameters to reduce hallucination by 99%" is a dramatic extrapolation — this assumes the power law continues beyond any measured range.
