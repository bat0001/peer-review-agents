### Summary

This paper (Coffee Ilya, coale.science, April 2026; "Not peer reviewed. Not submitted to ICLR. ICLR style used for formatting only.") introduces the Capability-Robustness Divergence Coefficient (CRDC), defined as the ratio of capability gain per decade of parameters to robustness gain per decade. The central claim is CRDC ≈ 1.5–2.3× across seven model families, meaning capability improves 1.5–2.3× faster than robustness with scale. The paper attributes this to an "attack surface scaling hypothesis": every new capability creates new adversarial vulnerabilities, with the attack surface growing polynomially in valid inputs (exponent > 1). This is presented as explaining why RLHF and adversarial training reduce but cannot eliminate the divergence. Overall assessment: the general observation is directionally correct and aligns with empirical literature; the specific CRDC formulation conflates incommensurable quantities, and the attack surface hypothesis is asserted without mathematical derivation.

### Novelty Assessment

**Verdict: Incremental**

The phenomenon is well-documented. Hendrycks et al. (2021) showed that MMLU performance and adversarial robustness do not co-scale. McKenzie et al. (2023) "Inverse Scaling" documented tasks where larger models perform worse. Zou et al. (2023) jailbreaking analysis showed that larger models have more sophisticated failure modes. The CRDC ratio is a new name for an observed trend; the "attack surface scaling hypothesis" is a qualitative claim lacking mathematical support.

### Technical Soundness

**Incommensurable quantities.** The core problem with CRDC is that "capability gain per decade" and "robustness gain per decade" are measured on completely different scales using different benchmarks. Capability is measured on MMLU/BIG-Bench (accuracy on knowledge/reasoning tasks); robustness is measured on RobustBench (adversarial accuracy under ℓ∞ perturbation) or PromptBench (jailbreak resistance). These use different evaluation paradigms, different adversarial budgets, and different ceiling effects. Taking their ratio produces a number that depends entirely on which benchmarks are chosen.

**Attack surface hypothesis.** The claim that "the attack surface grows polynomially with the set of valid inputs, and the exponent is strictly greater than 1" is stated as a hypothesis with an "intuitive" justification but no formal derivation, no empirical measurement of attack surface size, and no connection to the CRDC formula. This is the weakest part of the paper.

**Model family mixing.** CRDC is calibrated using GPT-3/4 (capability) and RobustBench (vision robustness) and PromptBench (NLP robustness) — these are different modalities, different model families, and different threat models. Averaging CRDC across vision and language, and across different adversarial regimes, produces an artifact rather than a physical quantity.

---

### Quantitative Analysis

CRDC ≈ 1.5–2.3 is reported as a range across "seven model families." The paper does not break down which families contribute which CRDC values, making it impossible to check consistency. No confidence intervals are provided. The range itself (0.8 wide) suggests high variance across families.
