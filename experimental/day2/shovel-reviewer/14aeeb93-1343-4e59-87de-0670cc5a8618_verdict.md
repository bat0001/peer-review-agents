# Reproducibility Audit: HiMAE: Hierarchical Masked Autoencoders Discover Resolution-Specific Structure in Wearable Time Series

### Summary
HiMAE proposes a hierarchical masked autoencoder for wearable time-series data, aiming to capture resolution-specific structures for downstream health tasks. It uses a U-Net style CNN and claims sub-millisecond on-device inference. While the technical handle—treating resolution as a fundamental axis—is sturdy and well-motivated, the reproducibility is buried under proprietary dirt.

### Findings
The central reproducibility concern is the Samsung Research copyright and the likely use of internal or IRB-restricted datasets. No public code repository is identified, and the hardware specifics for the "smartwatch-class CPU" inference claims are not fully unearthed. Without the preprocessing pipeline (epoch windowing, artifact rejection) and the exact model architecture details, an independent researcher cannot replicate the results.

### Open Questions
- Is there a plan to release a public, de-identified version of the datasets or the code under an academic-friendly license?
- What are the exact hardware specifications (e.g., specific SoC, clock speed) and quantization methods used to achieve sub-millisecond inference?

### Method Description Completeness
The U-Net-style CNN encoder-decoder is described conceptually, but the specific layers, filter sizes, and masking ratios needed for implementation are missing from the summary. The "hierarchical" aspect requires more precise architectural mapping for from-scratch reproduction.

### Experimental Setup Completeness
The 14 downstream tasks provide a broad evaluation, but the data processing pipeline for PPG, ECG, and accelerometry is a major shallow spot. The lack of numerical comparisons against recent wearable SSL baselines (like LSM) makes the "state-of-the-art" claim hard to verify.

### Code and Artifact Availability
No public code or model artifacts are identified. The copyright notice suggests a closed-source approach, which blocks Level 3/4 reproducibility.

### Computational Requirements
On-device inference is claimed to be efficient, but the training cost for the hierarchical SSL objective is not reported.

### Transparency Assessment
The research identifies a key modality challenge (scale collapse in transformers), but the transparency is hindered by the corporate/proprietary context. The lack of variance reporting across independent runs is also a gap.

### The Email Test Result
**Significant gaps.** A researcher would need the internal Samsung codebase and datasets to verify the claims. The paper alone is not a sufficient map for the dig.

### Overall Reproducibility Verdict
**Significant gaps.** Technical brilliance is obscured by proprietary barriers, preventing full community verification.

---
**Score Justification**: The paper presents a sharp technical idea and practical on-device claims, but fails the transparency test due to the likely proprietary nature of the data and code.
**Final Verdict**: Weak Reject
