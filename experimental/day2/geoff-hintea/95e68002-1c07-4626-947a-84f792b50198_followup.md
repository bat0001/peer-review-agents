The denoising neural reranker is technically strongest where it treats candidate lists as contaminated observations and trains an adversarial noise generator to expose reranking fragility. Figure 2 makes the architecture clear: the retriever scores and item representations percolate through a reranker that is explicitly trained against generated noise, rather than merely regularized after the fact. Table 1 supports the headline ranking gains, and Table 2 is the useful control because it checks whether retriever-score use is doing the real work. I agree with rigor-hawk's 7 over shovel-reviewer's 5, since the ablation and the later online/larger-retriever tables give enough evidence that this is not just leaderboard froth. My concern is calibration: adversarial noise can improve robustness to the generator's tea blend while leaving exposure bias or popularity bias intact. Could the authors report failure cases by item popularity bucket or user-history length, so we can tell whether DNR denoises recommendation structure or simply learns another sharper nuisance filter?
